Tedla Brandsema

Software Architect & Independent Researcher

I am Tedla Brandsema, a software architect, engineer, and independent researcher based in the Netherlands.

My work sits at the intersection of software architecture, technical research, and long-horizon systems thinking. Through Cloudberry IT, I have worked on large-scale web platforms, publishing systems, content infrastructure, government and university websites, cloud-based applications, and bespoke software systems for organizations with complex operational requirements.

I began my career in professional publishing, where I worked close to structured content, XML, transformation pipelines, authoring environments, and production systems. That background still informs much of my thinking. I tend to treat information structure, system boundaries, and operational constraints as foundational design questions rather than implementation details.

Over the years, my professional work has included architecture, development, technical leadership, consultancy, and research and development. I have worked on systems for organizations including Leiden University, ANWB, the Dutch national government, the Ministry of Defence, Avans University of Applied Sciences, SDU, Kluwer, ARAG, and others.

Alongside that professional work, I pursue independent research and writing on artificial intelligence, machine learning, symbolic systems, software architecture, infrastructure, and the economic and structural dynamics of technological change.

I do not approach those subjects as separate disciplines. I am interested in the places where they overlap: where technical capability alters institutional incentives, where infrastructure changes economic behavior, where abstractions shape what can be built, and where seemingly narrow implementation choices produce larger structural consequences.

That interest has become more important as software moves toward increasingly agentic and autonomous forms of execution. In that context, questions of architecture, control, governance, deployment, and observability become less like implementation concerns and more like structural conditions for trust.

Much of my writing starts from that perspective. I am less interested in treating technological progress as a sequence of product announcements, and more interested in the forces underneath it: cost curves, control surfaces, deployment models, perception thresholds, dependency structures, and the way capabilities diffuse once they become reproducible.

This site is a public record of that work. It collects my essays, research notes, technical writing, open-source projects, and ongoing attempts to make emerging systems easier to reason about, and to provide the primitives that allow developers and organizations to build with similar operational discipline.
